What Is Psychological Pricing?

By Timothy Ware on September 28, 2021 Last updated on June 17, 2026

style
article-body

Psychological pricing relies on consumer behavioral traits to increase revenue.

Psychological pricing is one of the many pricing strategies employed by companies in an attempt to increase their revenue and/or profit. In the case of psychological pricing, the strategy is to rely on the behavioral traits of consumers to increase revenue.

While some psychological pricing strategies can be seen as manipulative, which might decrease sales in the long term as consumers see the company as dishonest, others are common price hacks that seem to be well tolerated by most people and effective.

Since no single pricing strategy is going to be sufficient in reality, the different forms of psychological pricing can all cooperate with your other pricing strategies, from bundle pricing to cost plus pricing, to ultimately optimize your prices.

In this article, we will go through what psychological pricing means, some common forms of psychological pricing, and the advantages and disadvantages of psychological pricing.

What are the different psychological pricing strategies?

Psychological pricing comes in many forms, but all of them have one thing in common: they are tricks. Now, not all tricks are equal, and they aren’t always deceitful, but they do rely on knowledge about human behaviors to make a price seem a bit more palatable.

Let’s look at some common forms of psychological pricing.

What are artificial time constraints in psychological pricing?

If you like heading to the mall every weekend, then you’ve probably looked in the front windows of every shop and thought “do they ever not have a sale on?” This is the essence of artificial time constraints.

These shops are creating the illusion that the current prices are ending soon when in reality the prices will largely be static over time—that “Back-to-School” sale ends just as the “Halloween” sale heats up, which gets you to the “Turkey Time”, then “BLACK FRIDAY”, and “Christmas” sales, which lead right into the “End of the Year Blowouts.”

A newer form of this can be found online, where websites claim the product is offered for only a limited time, but of course that time limit is fake.

Don’t be fooled by the countdown; it just resets when it hits zero.

This form of psychological pricing relies on “FOMO” or the fear of missing out. Nobody wants to miss out on anything, even if it is something you don’t actually want. At this point, it should be pretty clear why some people think psychological pricing is dishonest.

What is charm pricing in psychological pricing?

Charm pricing involves setting prices that end in .99 to create a left-side bias.

This is where everything ends “.99.” Researchers have found that, since people look at the price from left to right they have a “left-side bias,” which basically means that they don’t look at the numbers following the decimal point. A price of $1.99 is perceived by consumers the same way as $1 even though it is practically the same as $2.

Interestingly, the association of prices ending in 9s with value has led to an opposite effect where many people see prices ending in 0, or prices that do not have anything after the decimal at all, as prestigious. You often see this implemented at fancy bistros or restaurants.

What is innumeracy in psychological pricing?

Some psychological pricing relies on the fact that the average person has a tenuous grasp of math. Innumeracy (think illiteracy for numbers) is when someone has a lower skill level at math than is necessary to understand the difference between two numbers.

The classic example is when confronted with two sales, one stating “buy one get one free” and the other “50% off when you buy two items”, consumers think the former is a better deal than the latter.

While I’m sure every reader of this piece immediately saw those two sales are identical in volume and price, what if it were “buy two, get one free” vs. “30% off”? How about “buy one, get one half off” vs. “20% off”? The fact is, most people would need a few seconds to figure it out and might not take the time to decide.

This can be combined with artificial time constraints by making all of the sales practically the same final result but attractive to different people.

How does price appearance affect psychological pricing?

The way prices are presented can have a tremendous effect on the perceived cost of a product. Restaurants will use slightly smaller fonts for the price than the associated description to make the price appear slightly shorter, and therefore a smaller number.

You might have been in a store recently that uses a different color scheme for sale prices. For example, the normal prices are in black and white while the sales prices are in red and yellow. This naturally draws the eye to the sale items—which are precisely the items the store wants to sell you. Look closely at some of the sales too. Sometimes the flipped around regular price is the same or just slightly higher: regularly $8.99, now on for $8.96.

Finally, sometimes the cents are much smaller than the dollars to really drive people to think $8 when they see $8.99.

What are the pros and cons of psychological pricing?

All pricing strategies come with their advantages and disadvantages, and psychological pricing is no different. Most patrons aren’t going to care if you charge $99 instead of $100, and they probably won’t consciously notice if you shave a half point off the font size, but going beyond that can really upset people.

Especially in SaaS, you want to nurture the relationship you have with each subscriber so leaning too far into the psychological pricing model could be trading a small short-term gain for a loss of consistent long-term revenue.

What are the advantages of psychological pricing?

How does psychological pricing boost attention?

By offering constant sales promotions, you can expect to get more attention than usual. If you rotate among a few different sales promotions throughout the month that are effectively the same price but using different phrasing, for example “20% off” one week and then “Get three free months when you sign up for your first full year” the next week, you can expect to drive more customers to the sign-up page.

How does psychological pricing simplify decision-making?

One of the great advantages of psychological pricing is that it helps customers make decisions. For example, if you have a three-tier pricing scheme on your subscription site, then, by creating a “10% off” sale for the cheapest tier, “20% off” for the middle tier, and “30% off” for the most expensive tier, customers immediately see the added value of the top tier and it reduces their mental burden.

Can psychological pricing lead to high returns?

If done correctly, all those psychological pricing tricks can add up to a higher return. Even if the prices are discounted more than you’d like, the added volume of a really successful sales campaign can add up to more money in the bank.

How does psychological pricing create a sense of urgency?

Creating a sense of urgency, like limited-time offers, can drive purchases.

FOMO is real. This is coming from a board gamer who spends too much time and money on Kickstarter. (I actually ordered something in the middle of writing this… there was only 16 hours left!) If you can build that sense of urgency, while not overplaying it to the point it is no longer believable, then you can get people to make a purchase today instead of some day.

What are the disadvantages of psychological pricing?

Can psychological pricing be deceitful?

This is really the first, second, and third biggest disadvantages all in one. At its core, psychological pricing is preying on the fact that humans have evolutionary shortcomings in this high-paced modern world. While some of these tactics are so normalized as to be assumed, others are fresh, more powerful, and generally hated by anyone who notices them.

While running a campaign at the end of a quarter to meet your newest MRR target might not gain the ire of your client base, doing so constantly is bound to get people angry.

How can psychological pricing lead to misperceived value?

While having a “biggest sale ever” every day might induce FOMO in some potential customers, others might see it as an indication that your company is not doing very well—and therefore your service is probably not very good.

The way you price your service and the tactics you use to drive conversions can work both ways. You don’t want the psychology of your pricing scheme to backfire.

Is psychological pricing a long-term solution?

Ultimately, psychological pricing is best used sparingly when you want to hit a specific short-term sales target. In the long run, building a relationship through trust and honesty with your clients is how you can maximize their LTV.

Frequently asked questions

What is psychological pricing?

Psychological pricing is a strategy that relies on consumer behavioral traits to increase revenue, using techniques like charm pricing and artificial time constraints to influence purchasing decisions.

How does charm pricing work?

Charm pricing works by setting prices that end in .99, creating a left-side bias where consumers perceive the price as lower than it actually is, making it more appealing and increasing the likelihood of a purchase.

What is the goal of psychological pricing?

The goal of psychological pricing is to increase revenue by using techniques that influence consumer behavior, such as creating a sense of urgency or simplifying the decision-making process, to drive purchases and boost sales.

Can psychological pricing be effective?

Psychological pricing can be effective in driving sales and increasing revenue, but it can also be seen as manipulative if not used carefully, and may not be a long-term solution for building customer loyalty and trust.

How can businesses use psychological pricing?

Businesses can use psychological pricing by implementing techniques like charm pricing, artificial time constraints, and price appearance, and by using tools like Baremetrics to monitor and optimize their pricing strategies and track their effectiveness.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What is psychological pricing and how does it work? Psychological pricing is a strategy that uses human behavioral tendencies to make prices feel more attractive, without necessarily changing the underlying value.Rather than competing on price alone, psychological pricing tactics exploit how people perceive numbers, urgency, and framing. Common techniques include charm pricing (setting prices at $9.99 instead of $10), artificial time constraints that create urgency through limited-time offers, and price appearance tricks like smaller font sizes for the dollar amount. For SaaS founders, the key insight is that how you present a pricing tier can influence conversion just as much as the price itself. Used carefully, these tactics can lift trial-to-paid conversion rates without touching your underlying subscription price.
  • What are the most common psychological pricing strategies for SaaS subscription businesses? The most widely used psychological pricing strategies in SaaS are charm pricing, tiered discount framing, and urgency-based promotions tied to billing intervals.Here are the tactics most relevant to subscription businesses:Charm pricing: pricing plans at $49/month instead of $50 to trigger left-side bias in readersTiered discount framing: offering 10%, 20%, and 30% off ascending pricing tiers to steer customers toward higher plansArtificial urgency: limited-time annual plan discounts that drive customers to commit upfront, reducing churn riskInnumeracy plays: framing the same deal as "3 months free" instead of "25% off" to make it feel more generousEach tactic works best when layered with a clear value proposition rather than used as a substitute for one.
  • What is the difference between charm pricing and anchor pricing? Charm pricing sets a price just below a round number to feel smaller, while anchor pricing places a higher reference price next to the real price to make it look like a deal.Charm pricing exploits left-side bias: a customer reads $99 as closer to $90 than to $100. Anchor pricing works differently by establishing a mental benchmark first. For example, showing a "regular price" of $199/month next to a current price of $129/month makes $129 feel like a win, even if the $199 price was rarely charged. In SaaS pricing pages, these two techniques are often combined: an anchored "was" price displayed alongside a charm-priced "now" figure. The risk is that sophisticated B2B buyers may see through artificial anchors, which can undermine trust with enterprise-tier prospects.
  • How can I use psychological pricing to increase MRR without damaging long-term customer trust? Use psychological pricing tactics selectively and tie them to genuine moments of value, such as annual plan upgrades or new feature launches, rather than running them as permanent fixtures.The tactics most likely to backfire are artificial countdown timers that reset and "biggest sale ever" promotions that repeat every month. These erode trust with the exact subscriber base you need to retain. Safer approaches for SaaS include framing annual billing as "get 2 months free" instead of "16% off," or structuring tiered discounts to make your highest plan the obvious anchor. Whatever pricing changes you make, track their actual impact on MRR, churn rate, and customer lifetime value in a subscription analytics platform like Baremetrics so you can see whether a short-term conversion bump is coming at the cost of long-term retention.
  • How do I run pricing experiments and measure the impact on MRR? To test psychological pricing changes, isolate one variable at a time, such as price presentation or discount framing, and track the effect on new MRR, trial-to-paid conversion, and churn rate over a defined period.Start by establishing a baseline using your current MRR breakdown: new MRR, expansion MRR, contraction MRR, and churned MRR. Then introduce a single pricing change for a segment of new sign-ups and compare cohort performance over 30 to 90 days. Baremetrics lets you track MRR movements in real time and segment by plan or acquisition cohort, so you can see whether a pricing psychology tweak is actually improving revenue or just shifting timing. Avoid running multiple experiments simultaneously or you lose the ability to attribute results to a specific change.
  • What are the main disadvantages of psychological pricing for B2B SaaS companies? The biggest risks of psychological pricing in B2B SaaS are damaged trust, misperceived product value, and short-term MRR gains that mask higher long-term churn.Unlike consumer e-commerce, B2B buyers are often evaluating your pricing page alongside a buying committee. Tactics that feel clever in a retail context, like fake countdown timers or inflated anchor prices, can make your product appear less credible to a finance lead or procurement team. There is also a value-signaling risk: perpetual "flash sales" can signal to prospects that your standard price is not defensible. Psychological pricing works best as a tactical lever for specific moments, such as end-of-quarter pushes, not as a permanent pricing posture. Your LTV depends far more on delivering genuine value than on how you frame the price at sign-up.
