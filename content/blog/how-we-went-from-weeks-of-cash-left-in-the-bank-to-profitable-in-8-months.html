How we went from weeks of cash left in the bank to profitable in 8 months

By Josh Pigford on March 07, 2017 Last updated on April 28, 2026

style
article-body

“Good morning everyone, I’m going to cut to the chase: We’re running out of money and I need all of you to take a pay cut.”

The team expected nothing. I had hinted at nothing. This came out of left field and a was a punch to the gut for everyone. We had just over 8 weeks of cash left in the bank before we’d all be out of a job and Baremetrics would cease to exist.But I’m getting ahead of myself a bit.

Starting the burn

In September 2014, we raised $500,000 in the form of a SAFE. I had bootstrapped Baremetrics to $20,000 in monthly recurring revenue and had only hired one person, but then found myself with a sizable chunk of cash sitting in my bank account.

At that point, Baremetrics was growing at a rapid pace, most months by 10–20%. So, what do you do when you’re growing rapidly and have a pile of cash burning a hole in your pocket? You spend it. Fuel for the fire, right? I mean, at that rate, we’d more than triple our revenue within a year, so it’d be downright foolish to not spend it!

And spend we did. We spent $250,000 in 120 days, in fact. Most of that on payroll as we expanded the team from 2 full-time to 6 full-time team members.

Within a year of the first investment, we were getting low on cash.

Feeding the burn

Justin Kan’s comment on spending VC money is spot on: “No matter how much you raise at your company you’ll end up spending it in 12–24 months.” And Mark Suster’s follow up to that rang even more true for us: “I would probably amend it to say 12–18 months. People do what people do. You have money, you spend it.”

So, we did what most funded startups do…we started looking to raise more money. Thankfully, our initial investor as well as one additional were willing to come in on the exact same term sheet as our initial funding. They certainly could have taken advantage of our situation and taken a big chunk of the company, but they didn’t.

In December 2015, we took on another $300,000 in funding to extend our runway…which we then used to hire 2 more people.

In hindsight, this sounds so crazy. I know that. Especially because I used the second round for hiring, knowing full well that’s what used so much of the money the first time around. I continued to be convinced that huge revenue growth was just around the corner.

Eternal optimism is a tough beast to overcome.

At this point we’d made the decision to expand outside of “Analytics for Stripe” to become a full “revenue analytics platform” that any business could use, regardless of their payment processor. That was going to be a huge engineering challenge, but would also fuel growth. So in my head, hiring more engineers just made sense because it’d help us with expanding our market which would in turn expand our revenue to cover our burn.

But the fact is, that just didn’t happen.

Stumbling upon a life boat I didn’t know we needed

Dave Ramsey talks about “nerds” and “free spirits” when it comes to finances in marriage. The “nerd” loves to manage the budget while the “free spirit” loves to forget there’s a budget to manage. My wife is the “nerd” and I’m the “free spirit”. She’s managed our finances for the entirety of our nearly 12 years of marriage.

It’s not that one is better than the other. Without the nerd, the free spirit would spend recklessly and go broke. Without the free spirit, the nerd would never spend any money at all and would be miserably uptight. They need each other for balance.

When it came to Baremetrics, I was running the company as a free spirit. I was playing “visionary” and just assuming things would work out because…of course they would! I have vision!Then in May 2016, a buddy of mine was telling me about someone he used as a sort of freelance CFO.

I’m fully aware of my free-spirit tendencies and had known for a while I really needed to have someone help me stay on top of the accounting side of things more (especially when it came to forecasting our profit & loss), but I’d free-spiritedly say I’d get to it “one day”.

That “one day” arrived and I sent a note to the folks at Forecast+. We had a couple of short conversations, I gave them access to all our financials and they jumped in to build a custom financial model so we could better estimate and predict where we’d be in the coming months and years.

Then I got a message from Jaakko (the CEO at Forecast+) asking me to hop on a call a week sooner than we’d originally planned as things weren’t looking so great.We hopped on a call, and this is what Jaakko showed me.

Burn rate forecast as of June 2016 showing us running out of money in spectacular fashion.

This, my friends, is not what you want to see. Ever. That little yellow line? The one that looks like it’s smiling at you as it implies impending doom? Things were about to get really unpleasant.

Course correcting

I had a call with Jaakko at Forecast+ on a Tuesday. That’s when he showed me that chart and we started talking about how to fix this.

We were net burning nearly $40,000 a month and needed to get that as low as possible or we’d run out of money.

I spent the rest of that week and weekend trying to think of every possible scenario and option for fixing this. Should I raise more money? Do we need to lay off part of our team? What about doing some sort of consulting work to get a small influx of cash? Would a bank loan help? What about maxing out credit cards? Could I afford to not get paid a few months? What if everyone took a pay cut?

I crunched every number I could think of and worked daily with Forecast+ to work through a myriad of different possible scenarios with growth and cutting expenses to figure out how we could right the ship.

A pay cut was the most viable solution.

Telling the team

On Monday, a week after finding out about our financial situation, it was time to talk to the team.Every Monday morning we have a video conference with the whole team (we’re all remote). We talk about what we did the prior week and what we plan on getting done in the coming week, but it’s mostly an excuse to make sure we all see and hear each other regularly. We spend most of the time just joking around and talking about random things. It’s always very lighthearted and laidback.

I start the video chat. Everyone is just hanging out, talking about the weather. I’m awkwardly quiet, waiting for the last person to show up. I’ve got my audio on mute and my camera turned off right until the last minute as I don’t want the team to see how nervous and on-the-verge-of-puking I am.

The last of our team shows up and I jump in.

“Good morning everyone, I’m going to cut to the chase: We’re running out of money and I need all of you to take a pay cut.”

I didn’t waste any time jumping in to the logistical details and didn’t really even pause because I was afraid someone would ask me a question or just be irate (even though that’s not the personality of anyone on our team).

I found out last week that we’ve only got a couple of months of cash left and after spending the past week crunching numbers and figuring out how to fix this, a pay cut makes the most sense as it allows us to keep everyone on board.I know that’s a lot to take in and is out of nowhere. We’ll forgo our usual standup this week. Take some time to think through and process all of that and please message or call me to talk through any questions and concerns you have.Thank you so much for all of your hard work and I’m sorry we’re in this situation, but I’m confident we’ll come out the other side stronger.

And that was the end of the call. I ended the meeting there and just sat at my desk sort of numbly staring at the screen waiting to see how our team would react.

Within minutes nearly everyone had messaged me, most asking for details. What exactly would their reduced salary be? What about benefits? A couple openly expressed their (rightful) frustrations at me. Some took the rest of the day off.

But at the end of the day, everyone took it about as well as I could have hoped, given I had let everyone down in a way that directly affected their livelihoods.

Within the first month we reduced expenses by nearly $20,000 with salary cuts and removing all unnecessary expenses. In the months after we continued to reduce expenses by as much as $30,000 a month.

Slow and steady with bumps in the road

The pay cuts I asked of everyone were intended to only last maybe 3–4 months. We were on the verge of expanding our available market, which our amazing team did a mere 8 days after being asked to take that pay cut.

I figured expanding to additional payment platforms and launching an API would have skyrocketed growth, letting our team get back to full salaries well before the end of the year.But very few outperform Gail Goodman’s long, slow SaaS ramp of death. We certainly continued to grow, but not really any faster than before. Just boring, consistent growth.

All time monthly recurring revenue

The pay cuts would need to stick around longer and one person on our team graciously offered to take a 50% pay cut for a couple of months to help get us to profitability.

Finally coming out of the woods

I asked the team to stick with the pay cuts until we hit profitability, at which point we’d start gradually decreasing the cut, and sure enough, our team pulled through and did it.In December 2016, we had our first profitable month since we took on funding in September 2014.

That was “reduced payroll” profitable, though. But at that point we started gradually reducing the pay cut and as of last month (February 2017), 8 months after I asked the team to take a pay reduction, the team is back up to 100% salaries.

We’re still right at the line where some months are profitable and others aren’t (thanks to annual subscription fluctuations), but as you can see in this updated burn rate forecast, that sinister yellow smile is a lot more inviting.

Lessons learned from nearly running the ship into the ground

The past 8 months have taught me more about business than the prior 15 years of being an entrepreneur.

Hire a “nerd” from day one

I mentioned before the concept of “nerds” ands “free spirits”. Figure out which one you are and if you are not the “nerd” who loves the accounting and bookkeeping components, then find someone immediately. Seriously. Today. Right now.

I waited far too long to do that, assuming my “vision” would somehow overcome, you know, reality. If you need a recommendation, I highly suggest the folks at Forecast+. They literally saved our business and have set us up with a really solid financial model that accounts for all the intricacies of building a profitable company (or becoming profitable, as it were).

Be a little doom ‘n gloom

Most entrepreneurs I know are, like me, eternal optimists. Unfortunately that optimism can be a little blinding at times. Force yourself to regularly run through some worst case scenarios and figure out how that would play out with your company.

The best way to future proof yourself is profitability plus 3–6 months worth of expenses as cash in the bank.

Get out of the startup rat race

Stop playing startup and instead focus on building a solid, sustainable business. That’s not to say all VC money is bad (I don’t believe it is), but far too many entrepreneurs take on funding thinking it’s the only way to build a business…and it’s not.

You’ll likely be much happier as a human being without the stresses of operating at loss while you wait for growth to catch up (which, for many companies, never does).

Hire slowly

If you’ve raised money or if you’ve just got lots of cash in the bank, the tendency is to spend it on hiring. The assumption is that hiring = faster growth. But the fact is, that’s rarely the case. Yes, in the long run having more people can lead to more growth but there’s a significant ramp up period for that (in some cases 6–12 months).

Everybody’s winging it

Last June, when I realized things were so dire, I started reaching out to founders I knew to get advice. It was unbelievable how many companies had gone through similar situations. In fact, the very week I found out our finances were trouble, Joel Gascoigne had just written about how Buffer was in the same situation (though on a larger scale).

That’s not to say I didn’t make mistakes (clearly I did), but understand you aren’t some epic failure because of your mistakes. The reality is, every founder makes mistakes along the way.

Doing what we want

Our team has been through a lot in the past 8 months as we’ve struggled to hit profitability. I’m unbelievably proud of the work that everyone has put in, especially after being asked to take a pay cut. That’s not what they signed up for, and they shouldn’t have been put in the position to need to take a pay cut, but they stuck it out, and for that I’m grateful.

In the past we’ve been driven more by the train barreling towards us, doing whatever we could to get off the tracks before it was too late. That manifested itself in throwing lots of things at the wall and just hoping something would stick, but now, we do what we want.

We do what we think is best for our customers, not what’s best for revenue. We grow within our means which means we’ll stick around, not being forced to call it quits because we’re running out of cash. We make our own rules and fully enjoy the ride.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • How do you turn a SaaS company around when you are running out of cash? Turning around a cash-strapped SaaS company requires an immediate, honest accounting of your burn rate, followed by a concrete plan to cut expenses before revenue can catch up.The most critical first step is getting real financial visibility, not just a rough sense of runway based on your bank balance. A detailed monthly burn rate model that maps your current MRR growth trajectory against actual outflows will show you exactly when cash hits zero. From there, the levers are limited but clear:Reduce payroll costs through temporary salary cuts or restructuring before layoffsEliminate every non-essential vendor, subscription, or tool immediatelyProtect revenue by addressing involuntary churn from failed payments, which quietly accelerates cash pressureBaremetrics gives founders real-time MRR, churn, and cash flow context so you can run these scenarios with actual subscription data, not spreadsheet guesses.
  • What is a healthy burn rate for an early-stage B2B SaaS company? A healthy burn rate for an early-stage B2B SaaS company is one that gives you at least 12 to 18 months of runway at your current MRR growth rate, with profitability achievable before that runway ends.The right burn rate depends on your growth velocity and your path to breaking even. A company growing MRR at 10 to 20 percent per month can afford more burn than one growing at 3 percent. But the danger is assuming that growth will accelerate to cover spending before cash runs out. The safer rule is to keep 3 to 6 months of total operating expenses in cash reserves beyond your expected profitability date. Tracking your net burn against your MRR movement in real time, rather than reviewing it monthly after the fact, is the most reliable way to catch problems early.
  • How can I measure and reduce involuntary churn caused by failed payments? Involuntary churn from failed payments is best reduced by combining real-time failure detection with automated retry logic and direct customer outreach before a subscription fully lapses.Failed payments account for a significant share of subscription cancellations and most of it is recoverable. The key is acting fast: a card that fails today is far easier to recover than one that has been expired for 60 days. Baremetrics Recover automates this process by detecting failed charges from Stripe, sending targeted dunning emails, and retrying payments on an intelligent schedule. This keeps involuntary churn separate from voluntary cancellations in your analytics, so you can track both accurately and see the true impact of recovery efforts on your MRR retention over time.
  • How do I separate new MRR, expansion MRR, contraction MRR, and churned MRR in my subscription analytics? MRR should be broken into four components: new MRR from fresh subscriptions, expansion MRR from upgrades, contraction MRR from downgrades, and churned MRR from cancellations, because each signals a different part of your growth health.Looking at total MRR as a single number hides the mechanics of how revenue is actually moving. A company can show flat MRR while bleeding churned revenue that is being masked by new customer acquisition. Breaking MRR into these components exposes that dynamic clearly. Baremetrics calculates all four streams automatically from your Stripe, Braintree, or Recurly data and surfaces them in a single real-time dashboard, so founders and finance leads can see whether growth is coming from acquisition, retention, or expansion without building a custom data pipeline.
  • How can I benchmark my SaaS churn rate against similar companies? You can benchmark your churn rate against comparable SaaS businesses by using open industry data segmented by MRR range, business model, and customer segment.A churn rate that looks alarming in isolation can be completely normal for your pricing tier or customer type. SMB-focused subscription products typically see higher monthly churn than mid-market or enterprise products, so comparing your number against an unfiltered industry average is misleading. Baremetrics publishes open benchmark data drawn from hundreds of real SaaS companies, letting you filter by metrics like ARPU and MRR band to see how your churn rate compares against businesses with a similar profile. This turns churn benchmarking from a vague gut check into a data-backed diagnostic.
  • What is the simplest way to share subscription KPI dashboards with investors? The simplest way to share subscription KPIs with investors is to use a live, read-only metrics dashboard that automatically stays current without requiring you to export and rebuild spreadsheets each month.Investors want to see MRR, churn rate, LTV, and net revenue retention on a recurring basis, and manually compiling those numbers from your payment processor is both time-consuming and error-prone. Baremetrics lets you create shareable dashboard links that pull live data directly from Stripe, Braintree, or Recurly. You send one link, it stays up to date, and your investors always have access to the same metrics your team is looking at. This also builds credibility because the numbers come directly from your billing source rather than a manually formatted report.
  • When should a SaaS founder publish their company scaling story or growth case study? A SaaS founder should publish a company scaling story or growth case study once there is a clear narrative arc with a real outcome, not while the story is still unresolved.Growth narratives for B2B subscription companies are most credible when they include specific metrics: MRR at the start, the inflection point, and the result. Vague scaling stories without numbers read as marketing copy. Readers in your ICP, typically other founders and finance leads, will immediately look for the data that supports the narrative. Publishing a SaaS scaling story too early, before you have an honest outcome to share, usually backfires. Publishing it with real subscription metrics, including uncomfortable ones like your burn rate or churn rate during the hard period, is what builds trust and earns genuine organic reach from founders searching for case studies like yours.
