What startups should know about Monthly Active Users (MAU)

style
article-body

Whether you are looking for investment from VCs or taking stock of the health of your start-up or app there are certain numbers you should keep your eye on.

Monthly active users (abbreviated as the acronym: MAU) is one of the numbers that can provide greater insights into the overall health of your business. In this post, we will look at why active users are important, what MAU is, how it can be calculated, and why it is a useful metric.

Why are active users important?

From a business perspective, it is important to know that your app is actually being used and is useful to your customers. Active users indicate that people are interacting with your service or product. A healthy number of active users is therefore a sign you are doing things right for your users.

Determining the number of active users over time helps with assessing the effectiveness of your marketing campaigns and the customer experience. It is also important in calculating other key metrics. For example, the lifetime value of customers cannot be calculated without knowing your retention rates, and customer retention rates rely on data on whether users are active over time.

So, to put it simply, the number of active users provides a measure of the general health of the business and serves as a basis to calculate other, more informative metrics.

Understanding Monthly Active Users (MAU)

Monthly active users (MAU) is the number of unique users who have performed some action in an app within the last month (30 days). MAU gives critical insights into revenue, and can be used to help calculate key metrics like Churn Rate, and Customer Lifetime Value (CLV), allowing you to better forecast your business's future.

Let’s dive further into this MAU definition and look at the three components that determine MAU.

Users: In calculating MAU, every user who performs any action in the app during a 30-day period is counted only once no matter how many times they have logged in and performed various actions.

Action: What actions qualify a user as an MAU? Is it a performing a particular action such as liking a Facebook post or logging in/opening the app? In most instances, it is opening an app, which allows for easier comparison to other apps and businesses.

Time frame: The last month or 30 days leading up to the date for which you are calculating MAU.

In other words, MAU can also be defined as the number of unique users who open the app within a 30-day period leading up to the date for which you are calculating MAU.

How to calculate Monthly Active Users (MAU)

Unlike other metrics, MAU provides an indication of an app’s performance over time, with performance based on being able to attract and retain users.

Why is MAU a useful metric?

It is important for start-ups to know their relevant metrics and KPIs, so tracking figures such as the ones below are important:

  • Monthly Active Users (MAU)
  • User Cohort Retention
  • Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)
  • User Churn Rate

For VCs looking to invest, knowing the MAU/DAU ratio is important. Why?

Because they are looking for indicators of stickiness (how many users are repeat users of the product) and signs of growth.

Paul Graham, cofounder of Y Combinator and a VC, says:

“If there’s one number every founder should always know, it’s the company’s growth rate. That’s the measure of a start-up. If you don’t know that number, you don’t even know if you’re doing well or badly.”

The best thing to measure the growth rate of is revenue. The next best, for start-ups that aren’t charging initially, is active users. That’s a reasonable proxy for revenue growth because whenever the start-up does start trying to make money, their revenues will probably be a constant multiple of active users. Revenue is typically measured in both Annual Run Rate, as well as Monthly Run Rate.

To measure the stickiness of your product, the Daily Active Users/Monthly Active Users (DAU/MAU) ratio is often looked at. It provides insight into the number of monthly active users who engage with your product in a day.

Important caveats

MAU is an easy-to-calculate and easy-to-compare metric. However, it does have issues, and some have talked about retiring the use of MAU. While retirement isn’t necessarily the best approach, it is important to be aware of these main issues.

It is an unreliable metric for just-launched start-ups

Putting stock in MAU early in a start-up’s life is a mistake. Given the definition of MAU, all of the promotional activities that are associated with a launch such as PR, being featured in app stores and publications, word of mouth, advertising, etc., can highly inflate MAU figures. It would instead be better to assess MAU once traffic has normalized over a few months.

Depth of usage isn’t accounted for

To qualify for MAU, according to some definitions, a user just has to log in and doesn’t need to engage with the product beyond that. So having a high MAU doesn’t necessarily mean that all those users are engaging with your product.From a monetization point of view, you can only monetize users who engage with your app. So it’s good practice to measure unique users who interact with a core feature of your product.

Quality of users isn’t accounted for

Not all users are the same. Users obtained from different sources tend to exhibit different engagement behaviors. Some sources, for example, may allow for installs quickly or cheaply, but if those users don’t engage with key features of the product then the source isn’t very useful. In fact, obtaining a large number of users from such sources only serves to inflate MAU numbers but does not provide much else in value.

What do changes in MAU mean?

Over time, MAU figures will change depending on the rate of acquisition of new users, retention rates of existing users, and the reactivation of lapsed users.

So, what do increases or decreases in MAU indicate and how can they be used by a start-up?

Increase in MAU

This tends to happen when the number of new users and reactivations is greater than the number of existing users that have churned.

(New users + Reactivations of lapsed users) > Churn of existing users

Sudden increases in MAUs can be driven by:

New users – A new advertising campaign, positive press, or the app being featured in the app store can drive an increase in downloads and new users; in turn, they drive an increase in monthly active users.

Reactivations – Start-ups that have a large base of users that are no longer active can reactivate them via email campaigns or push notifications.

Churn – Addressing issues which have put off users via a new release or feature can reduce churn rates among existing users. This, in turn, would help increase MAU.

Decrease in MAU

A decrease in MAU occurs when the number of new users and reactivations of existing users is fewer than the number of existing users that have churned.

(New users + Reactivations of lapsed users) < Churn of existing users

This is driven by:

New users – The number of new users may fall due to expiring subscriptions, reductions in advertising or promotions, or the app no longer being featured on publications or in app stores.

Reactivations – A decrease in reactivation or engagement campaigns can also result in a decrease in the number of reactivations

Churn – An increase in churn rates due to technical problems, features that put users off, or other issues can cause an increase in churn rates, which in turn lead to lower MAU.

What’s next?

Now that you know how MAU can be useful and how to calculate it, you can factor it into your user engagement strategy. Knowing how to predict churn, analyze churn and engage your users via email or in-app messages will help improve your monthly active users and overall user engagement.

FAQ

  • What is Monthly Active Users (MAU) and why does it matter for SaaS startups? Monthly Active Users (MAU) is the count of unique users who perform at least one action inside your product within a rolling 30-day period.For SaaS founders, MAU is one of the clearest early signals of product-market fit and user retention health. A growing MAU trend means your acquisition and retention efforts are working in sync. A declining MAU, even alongside rising sign-ups or downloads, often reveals a churn problem that revenue metrics alone will not catch in time. Paul Graham of Y Combinator puts it plainly: for pre-revenue startups, active users are the most reliable proxy for future revenue growth, because revenue will likely track as a constant multiple of that user base once monetization begins.
  • How do you calculate Monthly Active Users for a subscription product? To calculate MAU, count every unique user who completed a qualifying action inside your product during the 30-day window ending on your measurement date.Three components define the calculation:Who counts as a user: each person is counted once regardless of how many sessions they completed in the period.What counts as an action: define this consistently, whether it is a login, a core feature interaction, or another meaningful event. Using a core feature interaction gives a more accurate read on engagement depth than a simple login.The time frame: always use a rolling 30-day window, not a calendar month, to keep comparisons consistent over time.Track MAU alongside your churn rate and user cohort retention to understand whether your subscriber base is genuinely growing or just cycling through new sign-ups.
  • What is the DAU/MAU ratio and how do SaaS companies use it to measure product stickiness? The DAU/MAU ratio divides Daily Active Users by Monthly Active Users to show what share of your monthly user base engages with your product on any given day.A higher ratio signals stronger product stickiness, meaning users return habitually rather than occasionally. For B2B SaaS products, a DAU/MAU ratio in the 20 to 30 percent range is a common benchmark, though the right target depends on your use case. Tools used daily, like project management or communication software, should aim higher than tools used weekly or monthly by design. Investors and VCs watch this ratio closely because it distinguishes genuine product engagement from inflated MAU figures driven by promotional campaigns or app store features. Pair DAU/MAU data with your churn rate and customer lifetime value to get a complete picture of retention health.
  • What are the main limitations of MAU as a metric for B2B SaaS companies? MAU overstates engagement because it counts any qualifying action once, without capturing how deeply or how frequently users actually interact with your product.Three limitations matter most for subscription businesses:Launch distortion: PR, app store features, and promotional campaigns spike MAU artificially at launch. Wait for traffic to normalize over several months before drawing conclusions.No depth signal: a user who logs in once qualifies the same as one who completes core workflows daily. From a monetization standpoint, only engaged users can be converted or expanded.No quality signal: users from low-intent acquisition channels inflate your MAU count without contributing to revenue or long-term retention.Track MAU alongside cohort retention, trial-to-paid conversion rate, and MRR growth to avoid making decisions on an incomplete picture.
  • How can SaaS founders use MAU alongside MRR and churn data to benchmark business health? Combining MAU with MRR and churn rate gives SaaS founders a more complete view of whether growth is sustainable or artificially inflated.MAU tells you how many users are active; MRR tells you how much revenue they generate; churn rate tells you how quickly you are losing both. When MAU is growing but MRR is flat, it often points to a conversion or expansion revenue problem. When MRR is growing but MAU is declining, you may be retaining high-value accounts while losing smaller ones.
