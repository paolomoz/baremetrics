What is a Chief Technical Officer? A CTO’s Career, Job Duties, Salary, and More

style
article-body

If you live and breathe technology, it makes sense to find a job role where you can actively be part of the tech side of a business.

For some, this means embarking on a career as a software developer or in cybersecurity management.

Others, however, are more interested in the technology, including selecting, managing, and organizing the tech stack and overseeing relevant operations.

If that’s the case for you, working to become a Chief Technical Officer may be a good choice.

This guide will discuss everything you need to know about the Chief Technical Officer career, including common career paths, job descriptions, salary, and more.

What is a Chief Technical Officer?

A Chief Technical Officer— or “CTO”— is an authoritative figure that oversees the selection, implementation, training for, and management of a company’s tech and tech-related process.

This includes the tech side of the customer experience, cybersecurity, product development and delivery (especially for SaaS tools), and internal operational systems.

Suppose a company chooses a new DevOps deployment system or a SaaS billing tool. In that case, the CTO will often be involved and typically make the final decision.

CTOs are often C-suite team members, putting them in high-level positions within a company.

Chief Technical Officer vs. Chief Technology Officer: What’s the Difference?

Good news: You can use “CTO” to refer to either a Chief Technical Officer or a Chief Technology Officer because, in many cases, they describe the exact same role.

So, if you’re searching for a new job, you can try searching for both terms to see if it helps you find additional positions to apply for.

Chief Technical Officer Job Description: What Does a CTO Do?

CTO responsibilities may vary depending on the company and its operational structure.

In many cases, a CTO’s job description typically involves the following tasks:

  • Manages a company’s technology teams, supervising managers, setting goals for departments, and overseeing training programs
  • Outlining processes to achieve goals for the company’s research and development teams
  • Collaborating with other C-suite executives, including the Chief Financial Officer (CFO) and the Chief Operating Officer (COO)
  • Managing the company’s tech stack, tech processes, and tech training
  • Evaluating existing technology and systems to determine their impact and efficiency
  • Creating and monitoring the company’s IT budget and seeing how it fits into a company’s overall budget
  • Supervising processes to ensure DevOps best practices and cybersecurity, potentially including maintaining security compliance regulations
  • Evaluating the company’s existing tech infrastructure

The position of Chief Technology Officer has similar (if not identical) responsibilities.

How Much Do CTOs Make?

Data from Comparably found that the salary range for CTO positions starts at 140k and can go as high as 260k USD annually.

Some CTOs in high-performing enterprise companies can make upwards of seven figures.

They found that the average US CTO salary was $194,039 a year, with an average annual bonus of $60,654. This puts the average total income at around $254,000, though most CTOs make between $171,000 and $200,000 annually.

A Chief Technical Officer’s salary depends on multiple factors, including their experience, the company’s industry and size, location, and bonus structure.

C-suite positions typically come with annual bonuses, but make sure that your base salary is where you want it to be since bonuses are never guaranteed.

CTO Skills You Need to Excel in the Job

If you want to become a CTO, it goes without saying that you’ll first need to spend time working in other information and technology positions to get the experience needed. We’ll talk a bit more about that in the next section.

You typically need the following skills and traits to excel in a CTO’s roles and responsibilities:

  • Ability to manage a budget effectively
  • Leadership qualities
  • Strong communication skills
  • Organization and planning skills
  • Analytical, strategic, and data-driven
  • Willing to think outside the box
  • Capable of juggling many things at once
  • Able to see how technology fits into the business objectives
  • Knowledge of the information and technology, including new technology, policies, regulations, and best practices
  • Understanding of the customer journey and how tech can improve it and reduce churn rates
  • Willing to continually learn about the ever-changing tech field
  • Familiar with different programming languages
  • Strong hiring skills to find suitable candidates

How to Become a CTO: Common Career Paths

Working as a CTO (Chief Technology Officer or Chief Technical Officer!) is not an entry-level position, so you must work up to it.

A strong background in technology is crucial, and since CTOs manage entire IT and tech teams, understanding each person’s role is essential. Starting from the ground up is a good way to go.

Many CTOs follow a similar career progression:

  • Pursue Bachelor’s and even Master’s degrees like computer science or information technology
  • Start working in any of the following positions in entry-level roles: Web software development Information system management Network architecture Big data engineering Security engineering
  • Work your way up to higher-level roles, like senior software developer
  • Work in technology management positions
  • Apply for a CTO position after working in upper management on tech teams.

Having as much diverse experience as possible (or at least experience working with as many tech departments as possible) is a huge asset, as CTOs need to be familiar with all technology roles in a company.

What is “Chief Technology Officer As a Service?”

In some cases, Chief Technology Officers may develop their own companies. They’ll deliver “Chief Technology Officer as a service,” meaning companies can pay them as consultants. This may be more cost-effective for smaller companies that need the expertise of a CTO but don’t need someone on the payroll full-time.

Final Thoughts

Chief Technical Officers work in ambitious, respected, and typically well-paid roles. They play a vital part in the organizations they work for and can directly impact a business’s processes and progress toward goals. That’s an exciting role to participate in, so it’s worth working towards.

FAQ

  • What is a Chief Technical Officer and what do they do? A Chief Technical Officer (CTO) is the executive responsible for overseeing a company's entire technology stack, engineering teams, and tech-related processes.The CTO role sits in the C-suite and covers a wide range of responsibilities: evaluating and selecting technology tools, managing IT budgets, setting goals for engineering and R&D teams, and ensuring cybersecurity compliance. In SaaS companies specifically, the CTO often owns decisions around product infrastructure, DevOps practices, billing systems, and how technology choices affect customer retention and churn rate. They bridge the gap between technical execution and business strategy, working closely with the CFO and COO to align technology investment with revenue goals.
  • CTO vs VP of Engineering: what is the difference? A CTO focuses on technology strategy and external product vision, while a VP of Engineering focuses on internal team execution and delivery.The CTO role definition typically involves setting the technical direction for the entire company, evaluating new technologies, and representing the technology function at the executive level. A VP of Engineering, by contrast, is primarily responsible for how the engineering team operates day to day, hitting sprint goals, managing headcount, and shipping on schedule. In early-stage SaaS startups, one person often covers both roles. As the company scales past a few million in MRR, the responsibilities typically split into two distinct positions.
  • When should a SaaS startup hire its first CTO? Most SaaS startups benefit from a dedicated CTO once technology decisions start directly affecting product delivery speed, customer churn, or the ability to scale revenue.Early on, a technical co-founder often fills the CTO role informally. The case for hiring a formal chief technical officer becomes clearer when:Engineering team size grows beyond five to eight people and needs structured leadershipTechnical debt is slowing down feature releases and hurting trial-to-paid conversionCybersecurity compliance requirements arrive with enterprise customersThe founding team lacks capacity to own both product strategy and engineering managementFor smaller subscription businesses that need senior technical guidance without a full-time hire, a fractional CTO or CTO as a service model is worth considering.
  • What is a fractional CTO and is it right for a subscription business? A fractional CTO is an experienced chief technical officer who works with a company on a part-time or contract basis, providing senior technical leadership without the cost of a full-time hire.For subscription businesses in the $10K to $1M MRR range, a fractional or interim CTO can be a practical solution. They can audit your tech stack, set engineering hiring criteria, and help align infrastructure decisions with business metrics like LTV, churn rate, and monthly recurring revenue without committing to a six-figure base salary. CTO advisory services and fractional models are increasingly common in early-stage SaaS, where the need for strategic technical guidance is real but the team is not yet large enough to justify a dedicated C-suite role.
  • How much does it cost to hire a CTO at a SaaS company? A full-time CTO at a US-based SaaS company typically earns between $140,000 and $260,000 in base salary, with total compensation often reaching $250,000 or more once bonuses and equity are included.CTO compensation depends heavily on company stage, size, and location. Enterprise CTOs at high-growth subscription businesses can earn seven figures when equity is factored in. For seed-stage or early-stage SaaS startups, equity compensation often offsets a lower base. If budget is a constraint, a fractional CTO for startups can cost significantly less while still delivering senior-level technical strategy. Always anchor negotiations on base salary first since bonuses and equity are variable.
  • What skills does a CTO need to succeed at a subscription or SaaS company? A SaaS CTO needs a combination of deep technical knowledge, budget management ability, and a clear understanding of how infrastructure decisions affect subscription revenue metrics like MRR, churn rate, and LTV.On the technical side, CTO responsibilities require familiarity with DevOps best practices, cybersecurity, and modern programming languages. On the business side, the strongest chief technical officers understand how engineering choices affect customer retention and product delivery speed. Key skills include:Strong hiring instincts to build and scale engineering teamsAbility to translate technology decisions into revenue and cost outcomesStrategic thinking across infrastructure, security, and product developmentClear communication with non-technical C-suite executives like the CFO and COO
  • How does a CTO track the revenue impact of technology decisions at a SaaS company? The most effective way for a SaaS CTO to measure the revenue impact of technology decisions is to connect engineering output directly to subscription metrics like MRR movement, churn rate, failed payment recovery, and trial-to-paid conversion.For example, infrastructure downtime or billing failures translate directly into involuntary churn, a metric that is easy to overlook without the right analytics in place. Baremetrics gives SaaS companies real-time dashboards that separate new MRR, expansion MRR, contraction MRR, and churned MRR, so CTOs and finance leads can see exactly where revenue is being lost. The Recover feature automatically retries failed payments to reduce involuntary churn, closing a gap that many subscription businesses miss entirely. When technology leaders can connect their decisions to these numbers, conversations with the CFO and board become much more productive.
