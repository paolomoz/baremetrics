Sahil was born in New York to Indian immigrants. When he was four, they moved abroad to Singapore. At 18, he returned to America to attend USC.

Sahil was interested in starting his own business from an early age, and his founder journey began with building iPhone apps for startups around Silicon Valley. In fact, he attributes most of the rise of the big companies around 2010 to the invention of the iPhone.

One company that hired him to make their iPhone app was Pinterest. This eventually led to him being one of their first hires, and he stayed at Pinterest until he was ready to start Gumroad.

As mentioned above, Sahil always had the desire to build a company. This 10- or 15-year plan included some pretty common steps—get a degree, work at a big company, spend some time at a startup, and then move on to founding a company.

On paper, it was a smart way to go about this. However, just one semester into his time at USC he dropped out. As soon as he got to California, Sahil was taken with the entire Silicon Valley culture. It was the early days of the iPhone, and he decided to test out his mobile coding skills in the marketplace.

He used Hacker News to find clients indirectly. Companies that did not yet have iPhone apps for their startups would find his submissions useful and send inquiries to Sahil about building their iPhone app. These clients eventually included Pinterest.

“It’s weird because semester is not even a word people say outside of school. It’s like, ‘One semester, what? What even is that?’ Yeah, I was there for one, just from August to December. And honestly, I swear to God that I did not intend to leave when I got there. It wasn’t like … It’s funny because people look at my past sometimes and they’re like, ‘Oh man, you’re so smart. You started designing when you were young, and then you learned to code, and then you learned iPhone stuff, and then right before it blew up, and then you got a job at Pinterest because of that, and then you did this’.” – Sahil Lavingia

After delivering their app, Pinterest asked Sahil to join them as only their second hire. As the fourth person in the room, Sahil learned a lot with Pinterest. He found the entire period, right around 2010, incredibly optimistic.

First, the era of the smartphone led by the release of the first iPhone opened so many avenues for new ventures. Second, social media was slowly rising with a similar air of opportunity. Being inside Pinterest having created their iPhone app, Sahil had front-row seats to this new world.

“I would say, yeah, 2010 was weird. It was so different. It was so optimistic. It really was because of the iPhone. I would say it probably deserves 100% of the credit. And the later Android, but really iPhone started it. Instagram. Agora. Square. Pinterest. It really was … Slack I think started around … Snapchat. They were all really like late 2010.” – Sahil Lavingia

Sahil was witnessing a completely new set of behaviors. People were using their iPhones to create and their social media to share. This democratization excited him and formed the basis for Gumroad.

“It was really this idea that it was a new behavior, that I think could have enabled a whole new type of commerce, and content creation. Just like why the iPhone I think was so powerful. There was social media and everyone was getting a Twitter account, a Facebook account, Instagram account, Snapchat account. There were all these different ways to communicate online and there was no commerce. That democratization happened to text and all types of free content—video, audio, things like that—but it wasn’t being applied to paid content.” – Sahil Lavingia

The missing ingredient in this new economy was, well, the economy. People could create and they could share, but there just wasn’t any way yet to bring commerce into the equation. Nobody had figured out an answer to how people could actually profit from all this work they put into creating.

“And the idea was, could we make it as easy to sell content as it is to share content today? What would that do to the world? Would it be helpful? Would it create … ? Would it take all these people that have been sitting in their basements or their bedrooms making stuff but not being able to connect to an audience, and now they’re all of a sudden being able to connect to an audience but they still weren’t able to monetize properly. That was the idea that got me really excited about it. I didn’t know what it would do but it was like, ‘No one’s tried this. This is new.’ That’s what really drove me to be really excited.” – Sahil Lavingia

As the idea for Gumroad really formed, Sahil realized that he’d need capital to make it happen—a lot of capital. This was an idea that was going to require a lot of effort and a lot of buy in from creators to really take off. He wanted to find enough venture capital that he could spend five years really finding the community and market.

This proved easy for him as his time at Pinterest had earned him the clout needed to get VCs to go along with his long game. His first two seed rounds totaled about $8,000,000.

“Yes. Raising money was easy, yeah. I think the fact that I was an early employee at Pinterest, which had really just been noticed by VCs and had just raised a bunch of money.” Sahil Lavingia

While Gumroad is a huge success today, the path to profitability had some potholes. Their burn rate over the first year was much higher than expected.

Sahil brought this conundrum to the employees and asked for their input on what they’d rather do. Basically, his options were to keep all twenty employees for six months and hope to find profitability fast or to cut down to a team of five and keep going for two years. As difficult a decision as it was, the second option was selected, and it proved to be the right one.

“We’d burned a lot more money during those nine months and so we still had tie back. I was like, ‘We could lay off half the team or whatever, but then we might have to do it again.’ I wanted to be confident in the fact that we do this, and this leads to us controlling our destiny. It’s not like an awkward halfway point. It’s like we are really committed to this totally new way of thinking about Gumroad. And we need to be as invested in that as we were about being invested in raising money and doing that. If we just decide kind of to make the leap, we’re just going to fall right down it the metaphorical abyss.” – Sahil Lavingia

This decision didn’t come without a cost to the company. The publicity around the decision spooked a lot of users. A 75% staff reduction is often the first sign the company is closing. Since users relied on Gumroad for their income, many decided to find somewhere else to sell their content.

“I told people, ‘Look. We’re aiming for profitability. We’re going to get there. But it’s trust. If you want to leave Gumroad. If your business is on Gumroad. You’re doing $200,000 a year and you don’t feel comfortable anymore, then I wouldn’t hold it against you if you left.’ And people did leave. A lot of people did leave.” – Sahil Lavingia

Profitability has completely changed the mindset of Sahil and how he approaches his employees at Gumroad. When revenue is scarce, it has to be the first and only concern of the company. Without money, the company can’t keep operating.

Now that Gumroad is self-sustaining, he is encouraging his employees to go work on side projects, try new things, and really find out if there is anything they can create to help Gumroad and all the creators who use it.

“I’m sure the team would get upset at me, you know? When they’re like ‘Oh we have such a cool idea,’ and I’m like ‘But that’s not in line with what we want to build. That’s not in this direction that we’re going in and that’s the direction we’re going in because we need to raise money.’ Whereas now it’s like ‘Oh yeah, that’s cool. Yeah. Work on that in your spare time and you’re being paid hourly so it doesn’t matter if you do it on a weekend or in six months. Just keep plodding along and let me know how it goes’. And there’s that trust there, you know, that they’re not going to abuse that freedom, and it’s worked really great for us.” – Sahil Lavingia
